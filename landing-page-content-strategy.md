# PloverCRM Landing Page Content Strategy

This file is the content strategy source of truth for the PloverCRM homepage. It defines the purpose, message, visual role, and non-repetition rule for each section.

Use this together with [design.md](design.md) and [claude.md](claude.md). If there is a conflict, prefer the latest explicit user direction, then this file for content strategy, then [design.md](design.md) for visual system guidance.

## Core positioning

PloverCRM is a WordPress-first CRM for lead-based businesses.

The homepage should not position PloverCRM as a generic CRM or as a tool that generates traffic by itself. It should show how a WordPress website can become a lead-management and follow-up system after leads are captured.

Core idea:

> Turn your WordPress website into a lead machine.

Supporting idea:

> Your website can do more than look professional. PloverCRM turns form submissions, ad leads, webhook inquiries, calls, and other lead sources into organized CRM records your team can track, own, and follow up from web or mobile.

## Main content rules

- Do not repeat the same “capture → enrich → assign → follow up” language in every section.
- Do not use vague wording such as “lead lands in a notification.”
- Prefer concrete lead problems: scattered channels, unclear ownership, missing history, no reminders, lost source data, no next action.
- Do not use “after the click” as the main promise because it is too ad-specific.
- Use “after form submission,” “after capture,” or “after the lead comes in” when clarity is needed.
- Do not use duplicate chips in the hero.
- Do not place a full integration logo carousel in the hero. Keep integration proof for the integrations section.
- Use metric-style benefit cards, but avoid unsupported guaranteed claims.
- Treat Mobile CRM as a dedicated differentiator, not only a small feature tab.
- Objection handling and FAQ must look and read differently.
- Avoid “lead generation machine” wording because PloverCRM organizes, manages, and helps convert captured leads rather than generating traffic by itself.

## CTA direction

Primary CTA:

> Download Plugin

Secondary CTA:

> Try a Demo

Use “Download Plugin” because PloverCRM is WordPress-first and the visitor’s expected action is to get the plugin. The button should take visitors to signup, where they enter email/password and then immediately receive access to the 30-day trial plugin download.

Use “Try a Demo” for the live interactive demo at `demo.plovercrm.com`. This is not a video demo. It is an installed demo workspace with sample data so visitors can experience leads, mapped fields, reminders, ownership, pipeline stages, and web/mobile follow-up instantly.

## Conversion microcopy rules

- Keep CTA support copy short. Do not add long explanatory lines near buttons, especially in the hero.
- Use one short reassurance line at a time, such as **No credit card required**.
- Distribute reassurance across the page instead of placing every detail in the hero.
- Use tooltips, hover details, or small helper text when extra CTA explanation is needed.
- Good CTA reassurance phrases include:
  - No credit card required
  - 30-day free trial
  - Cancel anytime
  - Works with your existing WordPress forms
  - Live demo with sample data
  - Set up your first source in minutes

## Time-to-value rules

- Emphasize that PloverCRM does not require heavy CRM setup.
- The onboarding setup wizard should be positioned as a fast-start advantage: setup can take around 2–3 minutes for the first source.
- Visitors should understand they can start with one source and add more later.
- Visitors can start with a WordPress form, Facebook Lead Ads, Google Ads, webhook, or another supported source depending on their workflow.
- Avoid making setup sound like a migration project.

## Cognitive load rules

- The live page should expose only the minimum copy needed for decision-making.
- Prefer visuals, screenshots, cards, tabs, tooltips, and progressive disclosure over long text blocks.
- Do not show every feature, integration, or detail at once.
- Section 3 and Section 5 can use tooltips for secondary details.
- Integrations should show priority items first, with a **View More** option for the full list.
- Product pillars should not show all seven tabs as large equal elements at once. Show 2–3 priority items first and reveal or scroll the rest.
- Live Lead Record Anatomy should use screenshots, zoomed fragments, and short labels instead of paragraph-heavy explanations.
- Do not make the homepage feel like product documentation.

## Urgency rules

- Use real operational urgency, not fake scarcity or countdowns.
- The urgency message should be that every captured lead without source, history, reminder, and next step can go cold.
- Add urgency as short lines inside existing sections instead of creating another heavy section.
- Good urgency directions:
  - Every scattered lead creates a follow-up gap.
  - Your next lead should not wait in an inbox.
  - Follow up before the lead gets cold.
  - Missed follow-ups often cost more than the system built to prevent them.

## Recommended SaaS conversion flow

The page should follow this persuasion sequence:

> Promise → Pain → Mechanism → Value → Compatibility → Features → Product Tangibility → Differentiator → Relevance → Proof → Objections → Decision → FAQ → Action

This order keeps the page focused on how SaaS visitors usually decide: first they need to understand the promise, then feel the problem, then understand the mechanism, then see value, compatibility, product depth, proof, and final buying reassurance.

**Visitor question:** What is this, and why should I care?

**Main message:** PloverCRM turns a WordPress website into a lead machine by organizing captured leads into clear CRM records with source tracking, reminders, history, next steps, and follow-up.

**Recommended badge:** WordPress-first CRM for lead-based businesses

**Recommended headline:** Turn your WordPress website into a lead machine.

**Recommended subheading/body direction:** Every form submission, ad lead, and webhook becomes a follow-up-ready CRM record with source, history, reminders, and next steps.

**Optional supporting line:** Your website can do more than look professional. It can give every captured lead a clear follow-up path from web or mobile.

**Hero copy rule:** Avoid using both a long subheading and long body paragraph at the same time. The live hero should feel sharp, not crowded.

**CTAs:** Download Plugin / Try a Demo

**CTA microcopy:** Keep this minimal. Use **No credit card required** near the primary CTA. Do not add extra demo explanation in the hero; the **Try a Demo** button is clear enough on its own.

**Visual:** Large product dashboard placeholder or product image. Keep it clean and focused.

**Do not repeat:** Do not show two rows of repeated source chips. Do not overload this section with integration logos.

**Reason for inclusion:** The hero defines the product promise and gives visitors a clear conversion path.

### 2. Broken Funnel vs Lead-Control Funnel

**Purpose:** Show the problem and solution together instead of presenting only a negative problem section.

**Visitor question:** What is broken in my current lead process?

**Main message:** Most leads are not lost at capture. They are lost after capture when data stays scattered and follow-up is unclear.

**Visual direction:** Funnel comparison with a separator.

**Urgency angle:** Every scattered lead creates a follow-up gap. The longer a lead sits without source, history, reminder, and next step, the colder it gets.

Left side: **Without PloverCRM**

- Leads scattered across email, Meta forms, WhatsApp, call logs, WordPress plugins, and spreadsheets.
- Source and campaign details get separated.
- No one knows who should follow up.
- No follow-up reminder.
- No complete lead history.
- No next action.
- No outcome feedback to ads or automations.

Right side: **With PloverCRM**

- Every channel becomes one CRM record.
- Source, campaign, and form data stay attached.
- Follow-up responsibility is clear.
- Follow-up reminder is visible.
- Lead details and history stay in one place.
- Stage and next action are clear.
- Outcomes can support automation and ad reporting.

**Future proof option:** A small real-proof strip may be added after this section later, but only when real proof assets are available.

**Do not repeat:** Do not use the same structured-record card from Section 3. This section is about comparison and contrast.

**Reason for inclusion:** It reframes the problem as a broken lead funnel and immediately shows the better path.

**Purpose:** Show the operational value using number-led cards early in the page.

**Visitor question:** What measurable improvements can this create?

**Main message:** Industry research shows why fast response, CRM visibility, mobile access, automation, and lead-quality tracking matter after a lead is captured.

**Visual direction:** Grid of number-led metric cards. Keep this section visual, concise, and easy to scan.

**Recommended metric cards:**

- **21x response advantage** — Responding in minutes can protect qualification chances before a lead goes cold.
- **10x contact advantage** — Faster follow-up helps teams reach leads while interest is still fresh.
- **74% better data access** — CRM users report better access to customer data when information is organized in one place.
- **65% quota attainment** — Sales teams with mobile CRM access are reported to hit quotas at higher rates.

### 6. Product Pillars Tabs

**Purpose:** Explain the core capabilities of PloverCRM in an interactive, digestible way after the visitor understands value and compatibility.

**Visitor question:** What does the product actually do?

**Main message:** PloverCRM captures, enriches, assigns, tracks, reminds, automates, and preserves lead history.

**Recommended tab count:** 7 tabs maximum. More than that risks becoming documentation instead of marketing.

**Progressive disclosure rule:** Do not show all seven tabs as large equal elements at once. Show 2–3 priority pillars first, then reveal, scroll, or collapse the rest. Each tab should use a short title, one short sentence, and no more than three quick points.

Priority visible pillars:

1. Lead capture & field mapping
2. Contact enrichment & source tracking
3. Follow-up reminders

Secondary revealed pillars:

4. Lead assignment
5. Pipeline management
6. Automation workflows
7. Lead history & activity timeline

**Tabs:**

1. **Lead capture & field mapping**
   - Captures leads from forms, ads, and webhooks.
   - Maps custom fields into the correct CRM fields.
   - Reduces manual copy-paste.

2. **Contact enrichment & source tracking**
   - Tracks source, campaign, form, page, UTM, device, and submitted details.
   - Makes every record more useful before follow-up begins.

3. **Lead assignment**
   - Assigns leads manually or automatically.
   - Clarifies who owns the next step.

4. **Pipeline management**
   - Shows where the lead is in the customer journey.
   - Helps teams prioritize active opportunities.

5. **Follow-up reminders**
   - Keeps next actions visible.
   - Helps prevent leads from going cold.

6. **Automation workflows**
   - Triggers notifications, templates, outgoing webhooks, ad outcome routing, and workflow actions.

7. **Lead history & activity timeline**
   - Keeps notes, status changes, reminders, communication history, and activity in one place.

**Do not include as only a tab:** Mobile CRM. It is important enough for a dedicated section.

**Do not repeat:** Do not explain the entire lead journey again. Each tab should focus on one capability.

**Reason for inclusion:** It proves the product has depth without overwhelming the visitor.

**Recommended headline:** Every lead record shows the full story.

Central visual:

- Large lead details page screenshot or mockup.
- The screenshot should be the hero of the section.
- It should show a realistic lead record with contact details, source, owner, stage, reminder, notes, history, and next action.

Surrounding zoom cards / callouts should use very short labels:

1. **Source**
2. **Submitted details**

### 11. Objection Handling

**Purpose:** Address buying resistance before final conversion.

**Visitor question:** Why should I choose this instead of forms, spreadsheets, or a large external CRM?

**Main message:** Forms are not follow-up, and many large CRMs are too heavy for WordPress-first lead workflows.

**Visual direction:** Persuasive cards, not FAQ accordions.

**Recommended card topics:**

1. **“We already have forms.”**
   - Answer: Forms capture the lead. PloverCRM manages what happens after the submission. You do not need to replace your current forms to start building a better follow-up system.
   - Badge: Capture is not follow-up.

2. **“Do we need a heavy CRM setup?”**
   - Answer: No heavy CRM setup. No spreadsheet migration required. No switching your business to a whole new platform. Start with the resources you already have and connect one source first.
   - Badge: Start with what you use.

3. **“Will setup take too long?”**
   - Answer: The onboarding setup wizard should be positioned as a fast-start path. Teams can connect their first source in around 2–3 minutes, then add more forms, ad sources, or webhooks later.
   - Badge: First source in minutes.

4. **“Why not just use HubSpot, Salesforce, or GoHighLevel?”**
   - Answer: Large CRM and marketing platforms can be powerful, but many WordPress businesses do not need a heavy external system just to control website leads. PloverCRM is focused on the WordPress lead workflow: capture, source, stage, reminder, history, mobile access, and outcome visibility.
   - Badge: Focused WordPress lead CRM.

5. **“Is PloverCRM a replacement for those platforms?”**
   - Answer: Not always. If a business needs enterprise sales operations, large marketing suites, or complex multi-department CRM processes, a larger platform may still make sense. PloverCRM is for teams that want a cleaner WordPress-first lead-control system without moving every lead workflow into a broad, expensive external platform.
   - Badge: Not bloated. Not generic.

6. **“Will this cost more than it saves?”**
   - Answer: Missed leads, slow response, and unclear ownership often cost more than the CRM itself. PloverCRM should be positioned as a practical recovery tool for opportunities that were already being captured but not properly followed up.
   - Badge: Recover more opportunities.

7. **“Who owns the data?”**
   - Answer: PloverCRM keeps the lead workflow closer to your WordPress business instead of forcing every lead into a disconnected external system. This supports a stronger ownership message for teams that want more control over their website lead data and workflow.
   - Badge: More control over lead data.

8. **“Will this be complicated?”**
   - Answer: PloverCRM focuses on the lead workflow: source, stage, reminder, next action, and history. The page should contrast this focused workflow against heavy CRMs that can require setup, training, pipelines, marketing modules, and sales operations complexity before a small team gets value.
   - Badge: Simple lead control.

**Comparative positioning rule:** Do not claim PloverCRM is better than HubSpot, Salesforce, or GoHighLevel for every customer. Position it as the focused WordPress ecosystem alternative for businesses that want to manage and convert website leads without adopting a broad external CRM or all-in-one marketing platform.

**Do not repeat:** Do not format this like the FAQ section. This is persuasion, not support documentation.

**Reason for inclusion:** It helps visitors overcome the reasons they might delay trying the product.
